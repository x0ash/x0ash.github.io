Ash's Hot Tech Takes

R.I.P Unreal

Written on 14/12/2022, Published on 14/12/2022

Introduction

Remember when I said that I wanted to post every Monday? That's gone well.

The website'll probably get updated off-schedule now but I've got a big post in the works that I should have released coinciding with The Game Awards, but instead I'll release it near the new year. That one's full of hot takes.

Anyway, here's a post I never thought I'd be making. Just an hour ago (as of writing), at 5PM GMT, Epic Games announced that they are shutting down the online servers for all Unreal games, alongside pulling them from every storefront. At the minute, you can no longer purchase any Unreal games on Steam. That consists of the following games:

  • Unreal Gold (1998)
  • Unreal II: The Awakening (2003)
  • Unreal Tournament: Game of the Year Edition (1999) [UT99]
  • Unreal Tournament 2003 (2003) [UT2003]
  • Unreal Tournament 2004 (2004) [UT2004]
  • Unreal Tournament 3 (2007) [UT3]
  • Unreal Tournament (2014) [UT4]

As of writing, GOG is safe, but I imagine it won't stay that way for long.

In an attempt to 'soften the blow', it seems like Epic have decided that one game will receive an update that will save it from being killed off completely. You might be wondering, "Which Unreal will they save?". UT2004, often regarded the best? UT99, still with active players before today? UT4, the latest and most 'relevant' one? Nope. All are dead. The only one that remains is UT3.

Why?

There is no suitable reason for Epic to be doing this. Unreal Gold and Unreal II are single player games first and foremost. They aren't impacted by disabling the master servers. The Unreal Tournament games also all have community servers that are still running now, even though the master servers are dead, so they're not greatly impacted either. The only impact is Epic pulling them from storefronts.

The more time I spend thinking about it, the less I understand the decision. By removing them from stores, it means that they're not making any revenue from these games whatsoever, so it's actually making them lose money. They mentioned in the blog post that they're considered 'out-of-date', and I can understand that for disabling the master servers. I don't understand pulling them from the stores.

Does pulling from the storefronts matter?

I think it's a fair point to make. I initially played UT99 through a pirated "borrowed" copy from the Internet, but I managed to pick it up on Steam before today. But that would mean that if I wanted to play any other Unreal game now, I would have to go through the same process, and I wouldn't ever be able to legally own a digital copy.

Fundamentally, it also makes the barrier of entry to these games larger. Truthfully, I doubt there's many people picking these games up for the first time these days, but if they didn't know where else to grab these games it makes life a lot harder for them. Furthermore, for some people, owning them on specific launchers is convenient - the main reason for me purchasing UT99 was the convenience of installing the Steam copy on Steam Deck vs. trying to figure out the correct Proton version to use.

Why not keep UT4?

This is where this article really starts to encroach on other articles I had planned. UT4 hasn't been updated since 2017 but was made with the full intention of being open source and available to the community to make better. I think that's a brilliant idea, but since launching in 2014, Epic seems to think otherwise. UT4 was the most up-to-date version of Unreal Tournament, and with it being open source, could be worked on by the community for years afterwards.

UT4, in particular, has received a special treatment from Epic in this situation. It only released on the Epic Games Launcher, which obviously Epic have full control over, while the others launched on other platforms. Because the other games are on alternative storefronts, the users retain access to the games even if the game is pulled from the store. UT4, on the other hand, will not be playable at all from 24/01/2023. This is absolutely vile behaviour, especially because UT4 was better than UT3 - the one they're deciding to keep.

It seems like their plan for keeping UT3 alive is by cramming the Epic social system into the game and hoping that it'll work correctly. That means it'll probably only be available on the Epic Games Store, meaning it'll be treated much like how Epic treated Rocket League (2015) and Fall Guys: Ultimate Knockout (2020) when they acquired developers Psyonix and Mediatonic respectively.

Why does this matter at all?

The Unreal games were influential on the games industry - it would be like if iD delisted DOOM (1993) because they considered it 'obsolete'. It sets a precedent for what could happen to other games released around the same time, and especially paints a picture for what could happen to Epic's current games.

Some developers have gone power-mad with their ability to control access to digital games, and especially in times where physical games are unfeasible due to their large file sizes making it difficult to fit on physical media, read times of said physical medias being too slow and with many machines having no way of reading physical media anymore, and Epic is the latest to join this evergrowing list. Every game, good or bad, should be considered a piece of history, and should therefore be preserved.

I want to write a bigger article on Games-as-a-Service (which will be difficult to do without just rewriting several of Ross Scott's videos), the importance of community servers and general scummy practices frequently popping up in the games industry, so this will be a very interesting case study to use for those articles. Hopefully in the coming weeks we can see a team form that work to keep UT4 alive (considering the game is open-source) and some hope for convincing Epic to return these games to stores.
