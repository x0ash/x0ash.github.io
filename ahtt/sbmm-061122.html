SBMM Is Killing Multiplayer... Or Is It?

Written on 06/11/2022, Published on 06/11/2022

Introduction

I didn't really want to make this the first article I wrote but it seems timely to get it out as soon as possible. Recently, Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2 (2022) has come under fire from some members in the COD community for it's inclusion of Skill-Based Matchmaking (SBMM).

For those unaware, SBMM is designed to match a player against other similarly skilled players in a multiplayer match. Historically, COD has included SBMM in its matchmaking since 2007 but recently it's algorithm has improved so that its effects are much more apparent. Obviously, one would think that a functional algorithm would be the best option, but veterans of the game take issue with this, after previously being able to join a multiplayer match and be thrown in with a wide range of skillsets.

Common responses to the debate against SBMM is that in previous generations of players, if they were to join a match and be unable to kill anyone, they would look up to the people with superior skill to themselves. Fair enough to this, I say. It seems a large amount of people making this argument started playing the game when they were young (we shall ignore the game's age rating), and so naturally the people much better than themselves would most likely be older.

In a similar sense, the older players in these times would enjoy playing the game and being able to quickly rack up kills to better their score. A big motivation to do this would be to obtain a higher level to show on their public profile. In this sense, they would be able to showcase their skill with a defined metric rather than having to engage in a battle with an enemy, which would have been particularly ideal when in an online argument about 'skill'.

With the now improved SBMM algorithm, players are having to work towards getting a higher level, whilst the game also becomes much more approachable for new players with no experience previously. I have my own personal opinions on SBMM, and I'll delve into them now.

My Opinions

I think, personally, the inclusion of SBMM definitely depends on the type of experience that you're searching for as a player. As a competitive individual, you may seek equal skill to yourself, as lesser-skilled individuals may not challenge you enough. For this, you may want SBMM.

As a veteran player, simply looking to get kills, you might not want SBMM. Allowing a range of skillsets in a single match allows you to equally obtain easy kills whilst also facing competition against those more capable.

However, as a new player, joining a multiplayer match and being repeatedly killed while trying to learn the mechanics of the game can be infuriating. In this case, even SBMM may not be the best approach. An environment has to be created to learn the fundamentals for the game where the player stays in complete control of their experience.

Games tend to do this these days via tutorials or cinematics. However, and it's time for a hot take:

MULTIPLAYER GAMES DON'T NEED TUTORIALS

In particular, I remember PlayerUnknown's Battlegrounds (2017) preventing me from playing the game with my friends until I did the mundane tutorial that didn't help me learn what I needed to learn in an expansive enough environment. I learned everything I needed to know about the game while playing the game, and without the help of my friend who was already an experienced player, I probably would have given up.

For an example towards this point, I will look towards a game that ironically labels a big tutorial as the "Single-Player" mode: Quake III Arena (1999). This is because it also demonstrates a much better way of introducing players to the game.

The Quake Experience

Quake III, unlike its predecessors, lacks a true single-player campaign mode. However, it provides a 'Single-Player' mode, which is effectively a sequence of pre-defined bot games for the player to advance through, with each game increasing in difficulty. This showcases one of the many features that older games had that modern games seem to ignore these days: bots only modes.

Bots-only modes allows the player to control the difficulty of their enemies. As such, if they have to learn how to use the weapons, they can guarantee that they won't be immediately killed after they spot an enemy. Additionally, some people (like myself) simply don't like playing against real players most of the time. As far as I'm concerned, I am an amazing Quake III player. Although, if I were to join a public server, I'd probably get slammed and have to come to terms that maybe I'm not amazing.

But when I first started playing Quake III, I knew that the game demanded you to learn the movement tech in order to be a competent player. This involves learning how to strafe-jump, rocket-jump and how to control your character at high speeds. In these conditions, where I wanted to focus only on the movement, I jumped into a random map (in my case, Q3DM6, which has since been my go-to map) and familarised myself with the game's motion. I didn't want to have to deal with people shooting at me, so I simply disabled all of the bots, and placed myself alone in a local server.

Some games these days still do this. Take Counter-Strike: Global Offensive (2012) for example. CS:GO stands as the highest concurrently played game on Steam on most days, despite being over ten years old. Though it may simply be a product of when the game first released (back when stuff like this was more standard), CS:GO allows players to do the exact same thing. In fact, it's considered a crucial part of a competent player's experience. Training maps (such as aim_botz) are used by professional players and amateurs alike, which make use of bots-only modes. Furthermore, custom training modes (created via developer console commands) often makes use of a lack of bots.

So why is it that games have stopped including this? Well, I think the initial point can help us here.

What's happened?

Our initial point focuses on SBMM's impact on multiplayer, especially in an environment where it previously hasn't influenced the multiplayer experience much. I think that we need to look deeper - what about the impact of skill-based matchmaking on... gaming?

SBMM internally always assigns a number value to the perceived skill of any given player. In hyper-competitive multiplayer games (like CS:GO for example), these values aren't kept internal but are instead displayed in an abstracted format as "ranks". These ranks are flexibly assigned to the player - a way of summarising their most recent performance. This is in contrast to levels, which will only ever increase depending on the performance in the most recent game. These ranks both in theory and in practice encourage players to try their hardest all of the time.

It's time for a new hot-take:

RANKS =/= FUN

My friendship group all almost solely play hyper-competitive games, and the one thing that matters most to them in the game is their rank. If they're not playing well, then their rank is at risk. If they're playing well, then their rank is going to increase. They hate going against players better than them, and love free kills where they're allowed to play how they wish. At the end of the day, however, they find no value in playing these games except for bettering their rank.

I, unlike my friends, don't like hyper-competitive games. I, myself, am competitive, but if there's one thing that I hate then it's the ranks. In my experience, I never find any sort of motivation from my rank. I don't particularly care about how I perform in comparison to other people. I care about the game's fun factor. For other people, it forces them to play. The goal state for other players is always to achieve the highest rank in the game, regardless of whether you actually deserve the rank or not. It creates an addiction to the game.

I think that's a crucial factor in the impact SBMM, and more specifically ranks, has taken on gaming as a whole - games aren't being designed for fun anymore. They're being designed as a vehicle for social status. There aren't games in the modern day like Quake III Arena or Unreal Tournament (1999) because the players looking at them won't see the fun or the charm - they'll be asking, "Where's my rank?".

I like to develop games in my spare time, and considering the resurgence of the style of the retro FPS in recent times, I've been developing my own arena shooter inspired by games like Quake III and Unreal Tournament. Occassionally, I'll show my games to my friends and ask their opinions. Upon showcasing my game to one of my friends in particular, this was the response: "Where's the progression? There's no point playing this game if there's no progression."

I think it perfectly showcases the flaw of ranks in gaming. Of course there's progression in terms of monitoring your own personal skill - I notice myself slowly getting better at the games I play every time I enter a game, even if I am only playing with bots. But there's a modern expectation for the game to not only tell you that you're doing a good job explicitly, but to also showcase it as a public trophy. I'd rather the focus of my game be the player's experience while playing the game; the fun factor.

The fun factor

I find fun in Quake's movement. I find fun in Unreal's iconic maps. I find fun being able to jump into either of these games and exercising my skill, no matter how sparse it is. I'm able to find the fun in the experimental phase of game development - the middle stage where the traces of what became can be found; where the developers threw everything at the wall to see what sticks.

The one thing that keeps me coming back to two games released over twenty years ago above any modern multiplayer game is that they're simply more fun. I don't need to worry about ranks. I don't need to worry about whether I'm too skilled for my opponents or if I'm not skilled enough. I just jump in and play.

SBMM may not be ruining multiplayer, but it's slowly killing games.
